The Data & Analytics Platform Zone is preparing for 2027 strategic plan reviews with Paul (CPO), Richard (CTO), and senior leadership. We are building the muscle to think beyond the immediate backlog and to form a clear point of view on where our areas need to go, why, and what it will take to get there. Strong operators become strategic leaders by practicing this, repeatedly.

Improve Cross-Team Collaboration
When every zone is working from the same framework and presenting to each other, we build a shared language, surface dependencies earlier, and stop making decisions in silos. The dry run process is as much about connection as it is about preparation.
🎤
Show Up Ready in the Room
Our teams do great work. They should be able to walk into any senior leadership conversation and represent that work with confidence, clarity, and conviction. That doesn't happen by accident. It happens through preparation, reps, and honest feedback before it counts.
🏥
Stay Grounded in the User
Our users are both external — clinicians, billing managers, practice administrators — and internal — the analysts, product teams, and engineers who depend on our data platforms and pipelines to do their work. Both matter equally. When we plan without starting from their reality, we drift. This framework keeps every user at the center of every decision, year over year.
The bottom line: A team that thinks strategically, collaborates openly, communicates with conviction, and stays anchored to the people it serves will consistently outperform one that doesn't. Regardless of how good the engineering is. This is how we build that team.

Section 0
0
North Star & Business Case New
Start here — the anchor for every section that follows

This section exists to prevent the most common failure mode in strategic planning: teams completing months of work against an end state that was never explicitly agreed to, then discovering — too late — that leadership had a different picture of "success." Section 0 locks the goalposts. Every section that follows is traceable back to what is committed here.

🎯

Why this comes first: When a project "isn't going as expected" or a team moves the goalposts mid-execution, it is almost always because the end state was never written down and agreed to. This section creates the anchor. Scope creep becomes visible. Expectation drift becomes arguable. If an assumption breaks, everyone knows it — because it was named here.

Five Required Components
SCR Narrative — Situation, Complication, Resolution. Half page max. The complication must name which users bear the cost — internal (athenahealth teams, analysts, engineers) or external (providers, practice staff, patients) — and what specifically breaks for them if the zone doesn't move in 2027.
Governing Thought — one sentence, specific, falsifiable. The claim the entire plan is organized around. Must be wrong-able: "we will make data more accessible" fails; "by end of 2027, every clinical workflow team will have self-serve sub-2s query access, eliminating the 3-day analyst bottleneck" passes.
North Star End State — 3–5 outcome statements that are either true or false by December 2027. Not activities shipped, not features delivered. Each must name who benefits (internal user, external customer, or the business) and by how much. Use the five-dimension structure: user outcome · business impact · platform capability · strategic position (optional) · operating model (optional).
Out of Scope — at least 2 things this plan explicitly does NOT address. If the plan focuses on internal users, name that external-facing outcomes are out of scope for 2027 and why. Naming tradeoffs proves real choices were made.
What Would Have to Be True (WWHTBT) — 4–6 assumptions that must hold for the plan to succeed, each with a stated consequence if false. This is goalpost protection: when something goes sideways later, the conversation becomes "which assumption broke?" not "the plan failed."
📌

The north star is the anchor — write it carefully before moving to Section 1. Your manager can check in on your HTML site at any time. Share it with them and ask: "Does my complication name who bears the cost — internal teams or external customers? Is my governing thought specific enough to be wrong?" You decide when to move forward — but the bar is high.

Section 2
2
Who We Serve & What They Need New
Current workflows, target workflows, and the delta — not just pain points

For a platform team, the output is always something someone else uses. Pain points tell you what's broken today. Target workflows tell you what "good" looks like when you've done your job. Every strategic bet in Section 3 must trace to a specific gap between the current workflow and the target workflow defined here. If that traceability doesn't exist, the bet doesn't belong in the plan.

Per Persona — Three Required Components
Current workflow — step by step. What do they actually do today? How many steps? How long? Where does it break down? Real friction, not categories.
Target workflow — what the ideal looks like when the platform works excellently. Step by step. How many steps? How long? What can they do that they couldn't before? What do they no longer have to do?
The delta — 2-3 specific gaps between current and target. These map directly to bets in Section 3. If a bet doesn't close a named delta, it doesn't belong.
Verbatim quote — real, attributed to role/context. "It's hard to find data" fails. "I spend 45 minutes every Monday hunting the right dashboard before I can answer a single question from my attending" passes.
🔗
Workflow → Bet Traceability
Before moving to Section 3, map every planned bet to the delta it closes: "Bet [X] closes the gap for Persona [Y]: [which delta]." If a bet can't be traced to a named delta, either the bet doesn't belong or the target workflow isn't specific enough. Fix one before writing the other.
✅

Model to follow: The XCCM slide in the Migrations deck. Real customer quote, specific problem, hard evidence (10,000 hrs/month, 3x dev support increase). That pattern of quote plus problem plus evidence is the standard — now add the target workflow and delta to complete it.

Section 3
3
Strategic Focus Areas & Key Initiatives Elevate
2–3 thematic bets, not feature lists — each tied to a user pain

Organize work into 2–3 strategic themes. Under each, define 1–3 key initiatives. Each bet must explicitly close a workflow gap named in Section 2 — not just reference a pain point, but name the specific delta between current and target workflow that this bet addresses. If a bet can't be traced to a workflow gap, it doesn't belong.

Per Initiative
Plain-language description — what it is and why it matters
Which workflow gap it closes — name the persona, the delta from Section 2, and how this bet bridges it
Technical solution or approach, named specifically and framed as an enabler rather than a deliverable
Expected outcome — user benefit and business impact
Who else is impacted — internal teams, other zones, or external customers affected by this work, even if they don't own it
What depends on others — work in other zones or subdivisions that must happen for this initiative to succeed
💡

The full picture test: For every initiative, ask who finds out about this work when it ships rather than while it's being planned. If the answer is anyone, that's a coordination gap. Requirements written without accounting for upstream dependencies or downstream impacts are incomplete by definition.

⚠️

Common failure mode to watch for: Technical details presented as OKRs or strategic outcomes. "POC on OGG for Postgres as an alternative to Debezium" is an engineering activity, not a strategic initiative. The correct framing is: "We are reducing pipeline ingestion overhead by 30% to support growing demand from internal teams." The technical approach (OGG vs. Debezium evaluation) is one sentence underneath it. The solution serves the outcome. It never replaces it.

Section 4
4
Investment, Capacity & Expected Return New
Every initiative must be sized — vague scope leads to vague investment decisions

Budget and capacity are not finance team concerns. They are product leadership concerns. If a plan cannot articulate what an initiative costs in engineering hours and what it is expected to return, it is not ready to be prioritized. This section does not require precise estimates. It requires honest ones.

Technical Specificity
"Introducing AI and automation" is not a technical solution. Name the tool, the approach, and the scope. For example: "We are building an AI-powered mapping agent using Cortex, targeting the 3 highest-volume data types, reducing manual mapping time from 4 hours to under 30 minutes per project."
Level of Effort
Estimate engineering hours or sprints per initiative. S / M / L is a floor, not a ceiling. Where possible, break LOE into phases: discovery, build, test, rollout. If the LOE is unknown, that itself is a signal. It means the initiative is not scoped enough to be on the roadmap yet.
Expected Return
Quantify the return wherever possible: hours saved, error rate reduction, client retention risk mitigated, revenue enabled, pipeline reliability improved. If it is not quantifiable, frame it in terms of strategic value and explain what this unlocks that we cannot do today.
Required Per Initiative
Specific technical approach — tool, method, and scope (not "AI and automation")
Engineering LOE estimate — sprints or hours, phased where possible
Other resource dependencies — UX, data engineering, cross-zone, external vendors
Expected return — quantified outcome or clearly stated strategic unlock
Downstream impact — which internal teams, zones, or external customers will be affected when this ships
Upstream dependencies — what other teams must deliver for this initiative to succeed, and what the risk is if they don't
The Vagueness Test
Fails the test
"Reduce operational effort from 50% to 30% by introducing AI and automation."
No tool named. No scope defined. No LOE. No return calculation. Cannot be prioritized or funded.
Passes the test
"Reduce developer time on manual alert triage from 50% to 30% by deploying an AI-powered alert classification agent (Cortex-based) targeting our top 3 noisy pipeline categories. Estimated LOE: 6 sprints across 2 engineers. Expected return: approx. 680 false alerts eliminated per quarter, freeing 1 FTE equivalent for feature work."
💡

If the LOE is unknown, say so explicitly and name it as a dependency that must be resolved before the initiative can be committed. An honest "we need 2 weeks of discovery to size this" is far stronger than a vague estimate that quietly inflates once work begins.

Roadmap & Phasing Keep
Preserve existing format — add sequencing rationale

The release-tagged roadmap format with committed/not-committed distinction is strong. Keep it and standardize it across all zones. Add one thing: a brief sequencing rationale that explains why this order makes sense and what depends on what.

6
Success Metrics Elevate
Outcomes not outputs — with baselines, leading and lagging indicators

Separate metrics from the OKR status table and present them as a standalone success framework. Each initiative needs its own definition of success. Every metric needs a baseline. Without one, a target number is meaningless.

Must Include
Leading indicators — early signals that things are working
Lagging indicators — outcome metrics confirmed over time
Baseline today — where we are before we start
A "signal it's not working" metric — what triggers a course correction
Section 7
7
Risks, Dependencies & Asks Elevate
Name what could derail the plan — and what you need to prevent it

Elevate the existing challenges sections by adding an explicit "Asks" column. Every plan presented to leadership should surface one or two decisions or resources the zone cannot move without. This is where a zone leader asserts a point of view rather than just reporting status.

Must Include
Top 2–3 risks — specific, not generic
Mitigation approach or status for each risk
Cross-team dependencies with owners named, covering both what this zone needs from others and what others need from this zone
Explicit asks — decisions or resources needed from leadership
Impact map — which subdivisions, zones, or customer-facing products are downstream of this work and need to be in the loop
Coordination plan — how and when affected teams will be engaged, not just notified
⚠️

The "Asks" component is missing from all current decks. Challenges are surfaced but nothing is requested. A zone leader who identifies a risk and doesn't ask for what they need to resolve it leaves leadership with no action to take. Fix this.

⚠️

Cross-team impact is systematically underrepresented. Plans currently focus on what the zone owns, not what the zone's work touches. A platform team that ships without accounting for how Clinicals, Collector, Alicia's analytics team, or AES will be affected is writing incomplete requirements. The impact map is not optional.

Section 8
8
OKR Status & Operating Metrics Keep
Move to the back — detail supports the story, it doesn't lead it

Preserve the OKR table, operating principals, and wins/shoutouts. The only structural change is placement. These belong at the back. Leading with OKR status signals a reporting deck, not a strategic one. The story comes first; the detail validates it.

Part 2 · The Story

The plan is the what. The story is the why it matters. A well-structured deck presented without narrative conviction will not move a leadership audience. Every zone leader must be able to tell their story without the deck. The slides exist to support the story, not replace it.

🎯

Storytelling Standards — What Good Looks Like

1

Open with the tension, not the agenda

Don't start with "today I'll walk you through..." Start with the problem or the opportunity that makes this work urgent. The first 60 seconds should make the audience lean in, not settle in. For example: "Practices are going live with data that's 12 to 24 weeks old. That's not a technical problem. That's a provider experience problem. Here's how we're fixing it."

2

Anchor every strategic bet to a human moment

Before presenting an initiative, name the person it's for and the moment it changes for them. For example: "Right now, a billing manager spends 3 hours a week running reports they don't fully trust. Here's what we're building so they don't have to." Data and features are forgettable. A person and a moment are not.

3

Have a point of view — and state it clearly

The plan should have a clear thesis the zone leader believes and is willing to defend. Not "here are our priorities" but "here's our bet and here's why it's the right one." If someone pushes back, the zone leader should be able to say "I hear that, and here's why I still think this is right" rather than retreat into the slides.

4

Make the ask explicit and specific

Every presentation to leadership should end with a clear ask: a decision needed, a dependency to unblock, a resource to approve. "We need alignment on X by Y date or the roadmap slips" is leadership-ready. "We're working through some challenges" is not. Zone leaders should practice stating the ask directly, without softening it.

5

The deck is a prop — not a script

Zone leaders should be able to close the laptop and tell the story. If they can't summarize their plan in 3 minutes without slides, the story isn't ready. Slides should add visual support and data credibility. They should never be the thing holding the presentation together.

6

Anticipate the hard questions and answer them first

Leadership will ask: Why this over something else? How do you know this is the right priority? What happens if it doesn't work? What do you need from us? Zone leaders should address these in the plan itself rather than wait to be asked. Answering the question before it's asked signals strategic maturity and builds credibility faster than any slide.

Part 3 · The Dry Run Process

No one walks into a high-stakes executive review cold. The dry run process exists to give every zone leader multiple reps in safe spaces before they're in the real room. The goal is not a perfect first draft. It's fluency and conviction by the time it counts.

🔁

Three-Stage Dry Run Process

Stage 1 · Own Team

Safe Space

  • Present to your own team — PMs, POs, engineering leads
  • Goal: stress-test the plan foundation and story arc
  • Encourage honest pushback — does it hang together?
  • Director observes but does not lead the feedback
Stage 2 · Peer Zone

Constructive Challenge

  • Present to the other zone's PM team (cross-zone swap)
  • Goal: test whether the story lands for a less familiar audience
  • Focus feedback on story clarity, prioritization rationale, and asks
  • Simulate real Q&A — directors play devil's advocate
Stage 3 · Leadership Preview

Pre-Flight Check

  • Present to Jason (and optionally Bridget/Vijay)
  • Goal: final calibration before the real executive review
  • Structured debrief on content, story, and presence
  • Green light to proceed or one more round of refinement
Structured Feedback — What Reviewers Evaluate at Every Stage

📋 Plan Foundation

Is it grounded in real user pain? Are the strategic bets clearly tied to that pain? Are tradeoffs named? Are the asks specific? Does the roadmap follow the strategy? Is the full cross-team impact accounted for, covering both what this zone needs from others and what others need from this zone?

📖 Story & Narrative

Does it open with tension? Is there a clear point of view? Can the presenter tell the story without the slides? Does it answer the hard questions before they're asked?

🎤 Presence & Delivery

Does the presenter own the room? Do they hold their position under pushback? Are they confident with the data? Do they connect with the audience, not the deck?

❓ Q&A Readiness

Can they answer: Why this over that? How do you know? What if it fails? What do you need? Answers should be crisp, grounded, and not require re-opening slides.

💡

Culture note for Stage 1 and 2: The psychological safety of these early stages is what makes them valuable. Zone leaders should be coached to frame the dry run explicitly with their teams as: "I want you to push on this. Tell me where it's weak. That's the job right now." Defensive responses to feedback in the safe stages are a signal worth noting.

The three ELT tests
"So what?"

Does this matter? Is the problem real and urgent? Is the opportunity significant enough to justify the investment?

"How do you know?"

Is every claim grounded in evidence? User pain from real research. Market trends backed by data. ROI built from real estimates.

"Why you, why now, why this?"

Has the plan made a real choice? Named what it's NOT doing? Can every bet be defended over a reasonable alternative?

Get into Claude Code — Employee

Five steps. Takes under 5 minutes the first time.

  1. Open Terminal  Mac: ⌘ Space → type Terminal → Enter
  2. Clone the repo (first time only)
    git clone ssh://git@bitbucket.athenahealth.com:7999/~jakeith/strategic-framework-exercise.git ~/strategic-framework-exercise
    Already cloned? Skip this step.
  3. Create your branch — name it after yourself
    cd ~/strategic-framework-exercise && git checkout -b employee/[your-name]
    Example: employee/sagar-joshi. Do this once — return to this branch for every session.
  4. Launch Claude Code
    claude
    Command not found? Run npm install -g @anthropic-ai/claude-code first.
  5. Paste this as your first message — fill in the blanks

    Returning? Say "pick up where we left off" and Claude will read your session log and section files.
